UNIT 1: ANGULAR MEASUREMENT & THE UNIT CIRCLE (Weeks 1-2)

1.1 Radians vs. Degrees
The degree system (360 degrees for a full revolution) is an arbitrary historical 
convention inherited from the Babylonians. In advanced mathematical analysis, 
the radian is the natural unit of angular measurement. A radian is defined as 
the angle subtended at the center of a circle by an arc whose length is equal 
to the radius of the circle.

Conversion Formulas:
  - To convert Degrees to Radians: multiply by (pi / 180 deg)
  - To convert Radians to Degrees: multiply by (180 deg / pi)

1.2 Arc Length and Sector Area
Given a circle of radius r and a central angle theta measured strictly in RADIANS:
  - Arc Length (s): s = r * theta
  - Area of a Sector (A): A = 0.5 * (r^2) * theta

*Example Problem:*
A circular irrigation system has a sweep radius of 45 meters. The system pivots 
through an angle of 135 degrees. Find the length of the outer arc swept by the 
system and the total area irrigated.
*Solution:*
1. Convert 135 degrees to radians: theta = 135 * (pi / 180) = 3*pi / 4 radians.
2. Compute Arc Length: s = 45 * (3*pi / 4) = 135*pi / 4 meters ≈ 106.03 meters.
3. Compute Area: A = 0.5 * (45^2) * (3*pi / 4) = 0.5 * 2025 * (3*pi / 4) 
   A = 6075*pi / 8 square meters ≈ 2,385.65 square meters.

1.3 The Six Trigonometric Functions via the Unit Circle
Consider a circle with radius r = 1 centered at the origin (0,0) in the Cartesian 
plane. Any angle theta in standard position intersects the unit circle at a point 
P(x, y). The six trigonometric functions are explicitly defined as:
  - cos(theta) = x
  - sin(theta) = y
  - tan(theta) = y / x  (where x != 0)
  - sec(theta) = 1 / x  (where x != 0)
  - csc(theta) = 1 / y  (where y != 0)
  - cot(theta) = x / y  (where y != 0)

UNIT 2: ANALYTIC TRIGONOMETRY & IDENTITIES (Weeks 4-5)

2.1 Fundamental Identities
Every algebraic manipulation in advanced spatial modeling depends on these rules:

Pythagorean Identities:
  - sin^2(theta) + cos^2(theta) = 1
  - 1 + tan^2(theta) = sec^2(theta)
  - 1 + cot^2(theta) = csc^2(theta)

Quotient and Reciprocal Identities:
  - tan(theta) = sin(theta) / cos(theta)
  - cot(theta) = cos(theta) / sin(theta)
  - sec(theta) = 1 / cos(theta)
  - csc(theta) = 1 / sin(theta)

2.2 Verification Strategy
To verify an identity, start with the more complex side and transform it into 
the simpler side using valid algebraic and trigonometric steps. Never treat an 
identity like an equation where you move terms across the equals sign.

*Example Verification:*
Verify that: (sec^2(theta) - 1) / sec^2(theta) = sin^2(theta)
*Proof:*
Left Hand Side (LHS):
= (sec^2(theta) - 1) / sec^2(theta)
Using the Pythagorean identity sec^2(theta) - 1 = tan^2(theta):
= tan^2(theta) / sec^2(theta)
Convert to sine and cosine equivalents:
= (sin^2(theta) / cos^2(theta)) / (1 / cos^2(theta))
Multiply by the reciprocal:
= (sin^2(theta) / cos^2(theta)) * (cos^2(theta) / 1)
Cancel the cos^2(theta) terms:
= sin^2(theta)
LHS = RHS. The identity is verified.

UNIT 3: VECTORS AND SPATIAL GEOMETRY (Weeks 11-12)

3.1 Component Form of Vectors
A vector v in two-dimensional space can be represented from an initial point to 
a terminal point. In component form, v = <v1, v2>, where v1 is the horizontal 
change and v2 is the vertical change.
  - Magnitude: ||v|| = sqrt(v1^2 + v2^2)
  - Direction Angle: theta satisfies tan(theta) = v2 / v1

3.2 Dot Product and Orthogonality
The dot product of u = <u1, u2> and v = <v1, v2> is defined as:
  u . v = u1*v1 + u2*v2
An alternative geometric formula is:
  u . v = ||u|| * ||v|| * cos(alpha)
where alpha is the angle between the two vectors.
  - Critical Property: Two vectors u and v are orthogonal (perpendicular) if and 
    only if u . v = 0.

TOPIC: Right Triangle Trigonometry (SOH CAH TOA)

- Problem 1: In a right triangle, the side adjacent to angle θ is 8 inches, and the hypotenuse is 10 inches. Find cos(θ) and sin(θ).
  Step 1: Use the cosine definition: cos(θ) = adjacent / hypotenuse = 8 / 10 = 0.8.
  Step 2: Use the Pythagorean theorem (a^2 + b^2 = c^2) to find the missing opposite side: 8^2 + opposite^2 = 10^2 -> 64 + opposite^2 = 100 -> opposite^2 = 36 -> opposite = 6.
  Step 3: Use the sine definition: sin(θ) = opposite / hypotenuse = 6 / 10 = 0.6.
  Final Answer: cos(θ) = 0.8, sin(θ) = 0.6

- Problem 2: An observer looks up at a flag pole with an angle of elevation of 45 degrees. If the observer is standing 15 feet away from the base of the pole, how tall is the flagpole?
  Step 1: Identify the parts. The distance away is the adjacent side (15 ft). The flagpole height is the opposite side. The angle is 45°.
  Step 2: Use Tangent: tan(45°) = opposite / adjacent -> tan(45°) = height / 15.
  Step 3: Know that tan(45°) = 1. So, 1 = height / 15. Multiply both sides by 15.
  Final Answer: The flagpole is 15 feet tall.

- Problem 3: Find the cosecant (csc), secant (sec), and cotangent (cot) of an angle θ if sin(θ) = 3/5 and cos(θ) = 4/5.
  Step 1: Reciprocal rules: csc(θ) = 1 / sin(θ) = 5/3.
  Step 2: Reciprocal rules: sec(θ) = 1 / cos(θ) = 5/4.
  Step 3: Quotient rules: cot(θ) = cos(θ) / sin(θ) = (4/5) / (3/5) = 4/3.
  Final Answer: csc(θ) = 5/3, sec(θ) = 5/4, cot(θ) = 4/3


TOPIC: Converting between Degrees and Radians

- Problem 1: Convert 150 degrees into radians. Leave the answer in terms of pi.
  Step 1: Use the conversion formula: radians = degrees * (pi / 180).
  Step 2: Multiply: 150 * (pi / 180) = 150pi / 180.
  Step 3: Simplify the fraction 150/180 by dividing the top and bottom by 30: 150/30 = 5, 180/30 = 6.
  Final Answer: 5pi / 6 radians

- Problem 2: Convert 3pi / 4 radians into degrees.
  Step 1: Use the conversion formula: degrees = radians * (180 / pi).
  Step 2: Multiply: (3pi / 4) * (180 / pi). The pi terms cancel each other out.
  Step 3: Calculate: (3 * 180) / 4 = 540 / 4 = 135.
  Final Answer: 135 degrees

- Problem 3: Convert -60 degrees into radians.
  Step 1: Multiply by (pi / 180): -60 * (pi / 180) = -60pi / 180.
  Step 2: Simplify by dividing top and bottom by 60.
  Final Answer: -pi / 3 radians


TOPIC: The Unit Circle Explained

- Problem 1: Identify the exact (x, y) coordinates on the unit circle for an angle of 30 degrees.
  Step 1: Remember that coordinates on the unit circle are defined as (cos(θ), sin(θ)).
  Step 2: Look up or calculate basic values: cos(30°) = sqrt(3)/2 and sin(30°) = 1/2.
  Final Answer: (sqrt(3)/2, 1/2)

- Problem 2: What are the coordinates on the unit circle for an angle of 270 degrees?
  Step 1: 270 degrees points straight down along the negative y-axis.
  Step 2: The radius of the unit circle is 1, so moving straight down gives an x of 0 and a y of -1.
  Final Answer: (0, -1)

- Problem 3: Determine which quadrant the angle 5pi / 4 radians lies in, and whether its sine value is positive or negative.
  Step 1: Convert to degrees to visualize easily: (5 * 180) / 4 = 225 degrees.
  Step 2: 225 degrees is between 180° and 270°, meaning it sits in Quadrant III.
  Step 3: In Quadrant III, both x and y coordinates are negative. Since sine represents the y-coordinate, it must be negative.
  Final Answer: Quadrant III, Negative


TOPIC: Evaluating Trigonometric Functions

- Problem 1: Find the exact value of sin(240°).
  Step 1: Find the reference angle. 240° is in Quadrant III. The formula is: 240° - 180° = 60°.
  Step 2: Find sin(60°) from foundational memory: sin(60°) = sqrt(3)/2.
  Step 3: Apply the quadrant sign. In Quadrant III, sine (y-axis) is negative.
  Final Answer: -sqrt(3)/2

- Problem 2: Find the exact value of tan(135°).
  Step 1: Find the reference angle. 135° is in Quadrant II. The formula is: 180° - 135° = 45°.
  Step 2: Evaluate sine and cosine for 45°: sin(45°) = sqrt(2)/2, cos(45°) = sqrt(2)/2.
  Step 3: Calculate tangent: tan(45°) = sin(45°) / cos(45°) = 1.
  Step 4: Check quadrant signs. In Quadrant II, sine is positive and cosine is negative, making tangent negative.
  Final Answer: -1

- Problem 3: Find the exact value of sec(pi / 6).
  Step 1: Identify the relationship: sec(x) = 1 / cos(x).
  Step 2: Find cos(pi/6) [which is cos(30°)]: cos(30°) = sqrt(3)/2.
  Step 3: Take the reciprocal: 1 / (sqrt(3)/2) = 2 / sqrt(3).
  Step 4: Rationalize the denominator by multiplying top and bottom by sqrt(3).
  Final Answer: 2*sqrt(3) / 3

TOPIC: Amplitude, Period, and Phase Shift

- Problem 1: Find the amplitude and period of the function: y = 4 cos(2x).
  Step 1: The amplitude is the absolute value of the number in front: |4| = 4.
  Step 2: The period formula is 2pi / B, where B is the number inside with x. Here, B = 2.
  Step 3: Calculate: Period = 2pi / 2 = pi.
  Final Answer: Amplitude = 4, Period = pi

- Problem 2: Find the phase shift of the function: y = sin(x - pi/4).
  Step 1: Look at the horizontal shift inside the parenthesis: (x - C).
  Step 2: If it is subtraction, the graph shifts to the right. If it is addition, it shifts left.
  Final Answer: Phase shift is pi/4 units to the right.

- Problem 3: Given the equation y = -3 sin(4x + pi), identify its amplitude and horizontal shift configuration.
  Step 1: Amplitude is always positive: |-3| = 3.
  Step 2: Factor out the 4 inside the parenthesis to find the true phase shift: 4(x + pi/4).
  Step 3: Because it is addition, the shift moves in the negative direction.
  Final Answer: Amplitude = 3, Phase Shift = pi/4 units to the left

TOPIC: Law of Sines

- Problem 1: In triangle ABC, angle A = 30°, angle B = 45°, and side a = 8 cm. Find the length of side b.
  Step 1: Use Law of Sines: a / sin(A) = b / sin(B) -> 8 / sin(30°) = b / sin(45°).
  Step 2: Plug in values: 8 / (0.5) = b / (sqrt(2)/2).
  Step 3: Solve for b: 16 = b / (sqrt(2)/2) -> b = 16 * (sqrt(2)/2) = 8*sqrt(2).
  Final Answer: b = 8*sqrt(2) cm (approx. 11.31 cm)

- Problem 2: Why can you NOT use the Law of Sines if you only know all three side lengths (Side-Side-Side) of a triangle?
  Step 1: The Law of Sines requires at least one complete known ratio pairing an angle with its opposite side (like a and A).
  Step 2: If you only have sides a, b, and c, you do not have any angles, leaving every single fraction in the equation with an unknown variable.
  Final Answer: You lack a completed angle-side ratio pair to solve the equation.

- Problem 3: In triangle ABC, angle A = 60°, side a = 12, and side b = 10. Set up the equation to find angle B.
  Step 1: Apply the Law of Sines layout: sin(A)/a = sin(B)/b.
  Step 2: Substitute values: sin(60°) / 12 = sin(B) / 10.
  Step 3: Isolate sin(B): sin(B) = (10 * sin(60°)) / 12.
  Final Answer: sin(B) = (10 * 0.866) / 12


TOPIC: Law of Cosines

- Problem 1: In triangle ABC, side b = 5 cm, side c = 8 cm, and the included angle A = 60°. Find the length of side a.
  Step 1: Use Law of Cosines: a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc*cos(A).
  Step 2: Plug in values: a^2 = 5^2 + 8^2 - 2(5)(8)*cos(60°).
  Step 3: Calculate: a^2 = 25 + 64 - 80*(0.5) -> a^2 = 89 - 40 = 49.
  Step 4: Take the square root of 49.
  Final Answer: side a = 7 cm

- Problem 2: In a triangle, side a = 3, b = 4, and c = 5. Find the angle C opposite to side c.
  Step 1: Use Law of Cosines rearranged: c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab*cos(C).
  Step 2: Plug in values: 5^2 = 3^2 + 4^2 - 2(3)(4)*cos(C) -> 25 = 9 + 16 - 24*cos(C).
  Step 3: Simplify: 25 = 25 - 24*cos(C) -> 0 = -24*cos(C) -> cos(C) = 0.
  Step 4: Determine the angle where cos is 0: arccos(0) = 90 degrees.
  Final Answer: Angle C = 90 degrees (This is a 3-4-5 right triangle!)

- Problem 3: When should a student choose the Law of Cosines over the Law of Sines?
  Step 1: Look at the given information. Use Law of Cosines when given all three sides (SSS) or two sides and the angle trapped between them (SAS).
  Final Answer: Use when dealing with SSS or SAS triangle scenarios.


TOPIC: Solving Word Problems with Trigonometry

- Problem 1: A 20-foot ladder leans against a wall. The safety sticker says the angle the ladder makes with the ground must be 60 degrees. How high up the wall does the ladder reach?
  Step 1: The ladder represents the hypotenuse (20 ft). The height up the wall is the opposite side. The ground angle is 60°.
  Step 2: Use Sine: sin(60°) = height / 20.
  Step 3: Calculate: height = 20 * sin(60°) = 20 * (sqrt(3)/2) = 10*sqrt(3).
  Final Answer: The ladder reaches 10*sqrt(3) feet high (approx. 17.32 feet).

- Problem 2: A surveyor stands 100 meters away from the base of a building and measures an angle of elevation to the roof to be 30 degrees. Find the height of the building.
  Step 1: The ground distance is adjacent (100m). The building height is opposite.
  Step 2: Use Tangent: tan(30°) = height / 100.
  Step 3: Look up value: tan(30°) = sqrt(3)/3 (approx 0.577).
  Step 4: Calculate: height = 100 * 0.577 = 57.7 meters.
  Final Answer: The building is approximately 57.7 meters tall.

- Problem 3: A boat travels 12 miles directly North, then makes a 90-degree turn and travels 5 miles directly East. What is the straight-line distance back to the start point?
  Step 1: This forms a right triangle where the two legs are 12 miles and 5 miles.
  Step 2: Use the Pythagorean theorem to find the hypotenuse: c^2 = 12^2 + 5^2 -> c^2 = 144 + 25 = 169.
  Step 3: Take the square root of 169.
  Final Answer: The distance back to the start is 13 miles.

TOPIC: Pythagorean and Quotient Identities

- Problem 1: Simplify the trigonometric expression: sin(x) * cot(x).
  Step 1: Use the Quotient Identity to rewrite cotangent: cot(x) = cos(x) / sin(x).
  Step 2: Rewrite expression: sin(x) * [cos(x) / sin(x)].
  Step 3: The sin(x) terms in the numerator and denominator cancel out.
  Final Answer: cos(x)

- Problem 2: Given that sin(θ) = 5/13 and θ is in Quadrant I, use the Pythagorean Identity to find cos(θ).
  Step 1: State the fundamental identity: sin^2(θ) + cos^2(θ) = 1.
  Step 2: Plug in sine: (5/13)^2 + cos^2(θ) = 1 -> 25/169 + cos^2(θ) = 1.
  Step 3: Subtract: cos^2(θ) = 1 - 25/169 -> cos^2(θ) = 144/169.
  Step 4: Take the square root. Since it is Quadrant I, cosine is positive.
  Final Answer: cos(θ) = 12/13

- Problem 3: Simplify the expression: 1 - sin^2(x).
  Step 1: Rearrange the identity sin^2(x) + cos^2(x) = 1.
  Step 2: Subtract sin^2(x) from both sides to isolate cos^2(x).
  Final Answer: cos^2(x)


TOPIC: Solving Basic Trigonometric Equations

- Problem 1: Solve the equation for x on the interval [0, 360°]: 2 sin(x) - 1 = 0.
  Step 1: Isolate the sine term: 2 sin(x) = 1 -> sin(x) = 1/2.
  Step 2: Find the angles on the unit circle where the y-coordinate is positive 1/2.
  Step 3: This occurs in Quadrant I (30 degrees) and Quadrant II (180° - 30° = 150 degrees).
  Final Answer: x = 30° and x = 150°

- Problem 2: Solve the equation on the interval [0, 2pi]: cos(x) + 1 = 0.
  Step 1: Isolate the cosine term: cos(x) = -1.
  Step 2: Find the radian locations on the unit circle where the x-coordinate is exactly -1.
  Step 3: This only occurs at the far left side of the circle, which corresponds to pi.
  Final Answer: x = pi

- Problem 3: Solve the equation: tan(x) - 1 = 0 on the interval [0, 360°].
  Step 1: Isolate tangent: tan(x) = 1.
  Step 2: Tangent is positive 1 where sine and cosine have identical values and signs.
  Step 3: This happens in Quadrant I (45 degrees) and Quadrant III (180° + 45° = 225 degrees).
  Final Answer: x = 45° and x = 225°
